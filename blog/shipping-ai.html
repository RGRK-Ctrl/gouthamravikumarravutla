AI products · 8 min read

The demo is easy. Adoption is the product.

A good AI demo takes an afternoon. You wire a model to a plausible input, show it producing a plausible output, and the room lights up. Everyone can see the future. Then you try to ship it into an enterprise workflow, and it dies — not because the model got worse, but because a demo and a product are answering completely different questions.

A demo answers: can this work sometimes? A product answers: will someone trust this every day, on their real data, with their name on the outcome? The gap between those two questions is where most enterprise AI features quietly go to die.

Why the gap is wider for AI

Traditional software is deterministic. If it worked in the demo, it works in production — the behavior is the same. AI features are probabilistic. The demo showed you the good case; production shows you the long tail. A 90%-accurate feature sounds impressive until you remember that means one in ten outputs is wrong, and the user can't tell which one.

Users don't adopt accuracy. They adopt trust — and trust is destroyed by the failures they can't predict.

In enterprise settings this is sharper still. The person using the tool is accountable to someone. A wrong extraction on a financial filing, a hallucinated citation in a research document, a confidently incorrect answer to a compliance question — these aren't inconveniences, they're career risk. If a feature can embarrass its user, it doesn't matter how good the average case is.

What actually closes the gap

The work that turns a demo into an adopted product is rarely more model quality. It's the surrounding design:

Make uncertainty visible. A confidence signal, a "review this" flag, a way to see the source. Users forgive a system that says "I'm not sure — check this one." They abandon a system that's confidently wrong.

Keep a human in the loop where it counts. Not everywhere — that kills the value. But at the step where being wrong is expensive, an easy review-and-correct path buys you the trust to automate the rest later.

Design the failure, not just the success. What does the user see when the model can't answer? A blank, a wrong guess, and a graceful "no answer found" are three completely different products. The last one is the one people keep.

Instrument trust, not just usage. Track how often users accept the output unedited, how often they override it, where they drop off. Those are your real quality metrics — closer to adoption than any offline benchmark.

How this changes the roadmap

If adoption is the product, then the riskiest assumption is almost never "can the model do it." It's "will people trust it enough to change how they work." So I sequence launches to test that first: a narrow, high-frequency use case, with a design partner who'll tell me the truth, behind whatever trust scaffolding the workflow demands.

Ship the smallest thing that a real person will use on real work, and watch what they do when the model is wrong. That's the moment the product is actually being built. The demo was never the hard part.
